The Long Dispute

A renowned scientist --Bertrand Russell gave a public lecture on astronomy. He explained how the Earth orbits the sun and the Sun, in turn, orbits the vast collection of stars called our galaxy. At the end of his lecture, an old lady stood up and said "What you have told us is nonsense. The world is a flat plate supported on the back of a tortoise." The scientist gave a superior smile before replying"What is the Tortoise standing on?" "You're clever young man very very clever," said the old lady. "But its turtles all the way down!". Most people would find the idea of Earth balanced on a tortoise pillar rather amusing. But how can we say that we know better? How have we come to know that the Earth is Geoid in shape? Let us find out.


As early as 340 BC, Aristotle, the Greek philosopher put forward two good arguments to support the argument that the Earth was a sphere rather than a flat disc in his book "On The Heavens". Firstly, he realised that the eclipses of the moon occur only when the Earth is between the Sun and the Moon. He observed that Earth's shadow on the moon was always round. If it were a flat disc, the shadow would always appear elliptical and elongated. This could only be possible if the Earth were a sphere unless the eclipse occurred at a time when the sun was under the disc. Secondly, The Greeks observed the apparent movement in the position of the North Star (Polaris) when viewed from different locations on the planet. (To an observer located at the pole, the Pole Star would always appear to be directly overhead whereas to an observer located near the equator, it would appear to be just above the horizon). Thirdly, the Greeks also inferred that the Earth was spherical. Why else does one see the sails of a ship coming first and then the hull? From these arguments, Aristotle inferred that the Earth must be a sphere. However, he surmised that Earth was located at the centre of the Universe and that all the other planets, stars move around it.


Sadly, this misconception continued till 1543 until the Polish polymath Nicolaus Copernicus published his book "De revolutionibus orbium coelestium" (On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres) on his deathbed. This book was a major event in the history of science and sparked the Copernican Revolution. The Copernican Revolution was the paradigm shift from the Ptolemaic model of the heavens to the heliocentric model proposed by Copernicus. Copernicus's Heliocentric theory placed the Sun at the centre of our Universe. However, his theory stated that the sun and all the other stars were stationary. Yet again a century later, the Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe published a new cosmological model --the Tychonic system which combined the mathematical benefits of the Copernican system with the philosophical models of the Ptolemaic system. This, as you might have guessed resulted in utter chaos and no one, took it seriously.


India has long been known for its great knowledge and wisdom. We always come to know that Indian philosophers have discovered something long ago what Western scientists struggle to find today. It is written in the Puranas about the "Dashavatharas" that Lord Vishnu who is the saviour of the Universe, came down to save Mother Earth from all evil in the form of a boar and lifted her from the Cosmic ocean with his gigantic tusks. He can be seen as "Varaha" (boar in Sanskrit) lifting a spherical, blue-coloured Earth from the ocean. However, it is rather extraordinary to note that the Puranas emerged thousands of years before any of these events even took place! The word Purana means 'ancient, old' and it is a vast genre of Indian literature about a wide range of topics. The 'Puranas' were composed in the 11th Century BCE nearly 11000 years before Copernicus!
