Getting started on Astronomy

A beginners Ultimate guide for getting started on Astronomy


Giant balls of plasma held together by gravity, Spinning galaxies so far that we don't even know if they exist anymore, cosmic dust coming from Supernovas....
Space is awesome. And all you need to enjoy it is to look up..



Getting started

Step 1

Get to know the Night Sky

Familiarise yourself with the Night Sky. Go outdoors on a clear night, allow you eyes to get adjusted to the dark surroundings and then you're all good to go!
Gather a few Star Charts from your local library or download them from the internet and learn all the Catalogued Stars, Constellations. Just remember to have patience. Astronomy is a slow hobby and it'll take time to get acquinted with the basics. If you live in a densely light polluted area, here are a few Stars and Constellations that rule the night sky at any place on the planet. (Just remember to give your eyes enough time to adjust to the darkness of the night!)

Some of the brightest Stars:

  1. Sirius
  2. Rigil Kentarus
  3. Rigel
  4. Vega
  5. Polaris

Some of the brightest Constellations

  1. Eridanus
  2. Pavo
  3. Ursa Major
  4. Hydra
  5. Perseus

Step 2

First Telescope

Once you are confident that you have mastered the night sky, its time to buy your first Telescope. Now, you might want to consider shifting to a binoculars as it is quite portable as compared to a telescope and is easy to spot your targets, offers a wide field of view for first timers, making it easier to find your way around. In contrast, most telescopes offer upside-down views and it can often be confusing for a beginner. Binoculars are also very cheap as compared to telescopes and an ordinary 7 to 10 powered binoculars offer the same view as compared to an amateur telescope for just half the price. So the facts are here and its your decision to take.

Important things to remember

  1. Aperture

    2. The hole through which the light passes, usually the main lens or the mirror is known as the Aperture. The greater the Aperture, the greater the resolution. For beginners, a 6-8 inch telescope is ideal.


  2. Focal length

    3. This is the amount of light the lens or mirror bends gathered light into the focus point is the focal length. The larger the focal length, the smaller the field of view. So a telescope with a high focal length as 1500 mm is best suited for viewing closer objects such as the moon or other close planets. For deep sky objects, a focal length of 1000 mm is optimal.


  3. Magnification

    4. Although people hype about the magnification of the telescope, that's misleading. Making something larger isn't helpgul if the resolution is poor. Magnification is simply a measurment of the Focal Length of the telescope divided by the Focal length of the eyepiece.

Types of Telescopes

  1. Refractors

    2. The classic long and skinny telescope. Refractors bend incoming light through one curved lens and further into another curved lens just before the eyepiece. This simple setup makes refractors durable and they require low maintenance, but the high-quality lenses also makes them an expensive choice.

  2. Reflectors

    3. A great choice for beginners, it uses a two-mirror system to capture and focus light on the eyepiece. They usually are the best of the lot habing the largest Apertures making their resolution superb. They might be big and awkward, but they're most economical due to the cost per-inch aperture.

  3. Compound/Catadioptric

    4. A compact refractor/reflector hybrid that uses a corrective lens and curved mirrors to greatly increase the focal length. Compounds like Schmidt-Cassegrains, Dobsonians are more versatile and lighter than reflecting scopes, but their four optical surfaces significantly bump up their cost.

Keep an Astronomical Diary

Once you are comfortable around your telescope, start keeping an Astronomical Diary. Write down everything you spotted from the first day and note down all the important celestial events that'll be taking place in the next few months. It could be short and peppy, say "November 7th ‚Äî out with the 10x50 binocs ‚Äî clear windy night ‚Äî NGC 557 in Cassiopeia a faint glow next to two brighter stars".
